Kindness

Kindness - Being clear, having meaningful conversations, In the moment or close to the moment reinforcement of positive actions., Active listening, Ensuring the right person is in the right role ,care packages, thoughtfulness, unexpected acts of generosity ,Exiting team players through conversations and true respect for people retaining dignity and respect.

At both Hydro Tasmania and Marinus Link, I’ve had to lead through some very challenging executive dynamics. My approach has always been grounded in kindness as clarity, and accountability as shared purpose.

At Hydro, I had an executive who was brilliant—sharp strategist, expert negotiator, great with large customers. But internally, he was competitive to a fault—hoarding information and undermining his peers. I didn’t confront him with blame. Instead, I gave him regular, in-the-moment praise for what he did well, and gently asked reflective questions like, “How would it feel if someone treated you this way?” or “What impact do you think that had on the team?”

After a couple of months of these conversations, he came to me and said: "Caroline, I know what you want. But I’m a lone wolf. I work best on my own. What should we do?"

We agreed on a six-month runway to resign. That created space for accountability without ego—he stayed on, passed on his negotiation skills to three colleagues, and left with his head high. I later brought him in as a contractor on Marinus Link—he delivered superbly, with no team conflict. That’s leadership for me: protecting the person, protecting the team, and protecting long-term value.

At Marinus Link, I had another exec who was deeply capable—technically brilliant and loyal to me—but a bully to others. I’d heard this from several people and knew I had to act. I had many conversations with her, asking, “How do you think you show up?” and gently holding up the mirror. She couldn’t see it. Eventually, she asked if she could resign and leave with dignity over three months. I agreed. She handed over responsibilities with care. Her behaviour improved. It was one of the hardest decisions I’ve made—because she was so good at the job. But culture matters. Kindness isn’t letting things slide. It’s being honest, fair, and brave.

Kindness isn’t always a grand gesture. Often, it’s about being present, noticing what’s going on in people’s lives, and stepping in—quietly, meaningfully.

At Hydro, I regularly made time for unscheduled conversations—stepping away from the office, walking with someone, really listening. One of our analysts—a single mum—was struggling during COVID. I asked a colleague to help me cook a meal and deliver it to her. It was one of the best dinners I’ve had. Today, she’s the Executive General Manager of Strategy at Hydro.

That’s the impact of noticing.

Kindness is also creating space for courage. I had several female graduates reach out to me about sexist behaviour they’d experienced in the Asset team. They didn’t want formal action. They just wanted someone to hear them, to validate their experience, and to create a circle of support. So that’s what we did. We met, we shared, we strategised. And slowly, confidence grew. They didn’t just feel heard—they felt equipped. I wanted to fight their battles—I was angry. But those young women showed me something deeper: strength in strategy, and humility in how power can be used.

Kindness is making people feel seen. That’s where change begins.

Teamwork

Great teams aren’t just people grouped into a box on an org chart. They form when there’s clear purpose, clear expectations, strong leadership, and people willing to lean in.

At Marinus Link, I shifted our executive away from a focus on weaknesses—which had enabled some bullying behaviours—and toward one grounded in strengths.

That shift changed the energy. People owned their work, held each other to account, and asked for help early—because they trusted the team would show up. Our exec meetings became less about holding people to task, and more about asking, “Who needs support to get this done?” And celebrating our successes.

A few months before I left, we held a strategy day with a senior advisor. At the end of the day, she said she couldn’t tell who held what title—CFO, COO, CCO, PD— because we were solving problems as one team. There was clarity, respect, and laughter. And only in that moment did I realise how rare that kind of executive cohesion really is.

At Hydro Tasmania, there was no clear owner of long-term energy security for the state. I’m not exaggerating—there was simply a gap. With rainfall patterns shifting, customer growth rising, and new industries eyeing Tasmania, it was obvious the state would need more energy—and fast.

Before there was certainty around Marinus Link, I pulled together a small cross- functional team to help government understand the scale and mix of future energy needs. We called it Project Echidna.

The team came together through invitation—not instruction. A few key people from Commercial, Strategy, Legal and Comms put their hands up. We had a clear goal: deliver a compelling, evidence-based report to government by a fixed deadline.

We ran daily 30-minute check-ins. Everyone knew what they owned, when it was due, and how their piece fit into the whole.

It was one of the most rewarding team experiences many of us had. To this day, people still say to me, “That was so much fun working on Echidna.” A project born out of purpose, clarity and genuine collaboration.

At Hydro Tasmania, we needed to meet the five-minute settlement requirements—quickly, within the constraints of being a government- owned business, and on a tight budget.

There was strong pressure to outsource. But I backed our internal team. We built a small, focused unit where business analysts and developers worked side by side, with a clear roadmap, firm milestones, and a team culture of trust and delivery. We delivered the system on time and under budget.

Our independent auditor—PwC—said it was the best and most cost- effective solution they’d seen. They even asked to buy it. Great teams, with clarity and belief, deliver exceptional outcomes.

When I joined Hydro Tasmania, the 24-hour spot trading and generation dispatch team had the lowest engagement scores in the business—down in the low 20s. I worked two consecutive night shifts with them to understand what was going on.

What I found was a group that felt disconnected, underappreciated, and exposed—often working completely alone through the night. It had been that way for years, and no one questioned it. I did.

I pushed—first with the CEO, then the board—for change. We moved from single-person shifts to having two people on at night. We introduced a proper rest space with fresh linen, and I expanded the team to create flexibility and resilience.

Most importantly, we brought them into the heart of the Commercial team—gave them a voice, visibility, and context. By the time I left, their engagement score had tripled, reaching into the 60s. Because when people feel seen, supported, part of a team and connected to purpose—they thrive.

Integrity

Doing the right thing— consistently— even when it’s difficult, unpopular, or behind closed doors. It’s the alignment between your words, actions, and values. It’s not about perfection—it’s about consistency, honesty, and accountability.

I don’t play games or hide behind spin. Whether I’m working with ministers, boards, or my team—I lead with openness and clarity.

I never use complexity to obscure risk, and I expect the same from my teams. People deserve to know what’s at stake—and why it matters.

At Marinus Link, I inherited three misaligned governments, an ownership model built for conflict, and a global HVDC cable market running short on supply. The only way forward was trust—fast.

I set up a Working Group with all three governments, CEFC, and their advisors. We ran 64 meetings in 12 months. We were transparent, deadline-driven, and honest about risk. We did what we said we would.

There won’t be a history book chapter on the decisions I made behind the scenes to get it built.

But integrity is doing the right thing—even when no one’s watching. And I did. Every time.

Days before securing a Capacity Reservation Agreement with Prysmian, the Tasmanian Government tabled in Parliament that it no longer supported Marinus Link and was “drawing a line in the sand.” It was a high-stakes moment. I didn’t react emotionally.

Instead, I researched the drivers behind the announcement, and calmly reassured Prysmian and Hitachi that the Commonwealth remained committed and would still underwrite the agreement—despite not yet being an owner of the project.

I called an all-staff town hall and was clear about what I knew, what I didn’t, and how we would move forward. Because of the trust the team had in me, tension defused. Confidence held.

Our engagement score, measured soon after, came in above 70.

I created Australia’s first solar PPA not because it was trendy—but because it was viable, capital- efficient, and set a new standard for structuring clean energy deals for commercial customers

Leadership in a crisis isn’t about certainty—it’s about composure, credibility, and connection.

That consistency became our reputation—we were known as the team with integrity. It was a turning point. That trust was the foundation for making Marinus real.

Marinus Link required courage at every step. It was never about headlines—it was about doing what was right, and delivering sustainable outcomes.

Govt Stakeholder

My approach to engaging government stakeholders is grounded in transparency, understanding, and respect. These are the foundations for building trust— especially when navigating complex policy, regulatory and investment landscapes.

I focus on establishing open communication early, ensuring government stakeholders— both elected officials and senior public servants—feel respected, informed, and heard. Often, it's the relationships with senior departmental figures that enable momentum, clarity, and confidence in delivery.

I make it a priority to understand the drivers behind government thinking: policy objectives, political context, and community expectations.

Then I frame my approach to align commercial outcomes with these imperatives—without compromising integrity.

At Hydro Tasmania, I attended energy cabinet meetings alongside the CEO, directly briefing the Premier and key ministers.

At Marinus Link, I developed strong working relationships with senior figures in all three governments and CEFC—David Fredericks, Simon Duggan, Liz Molyneux (Victoria), Alistair Parker (Victoria)(will be referee), Craig Limkin (Tas), Mark Bowles (Tas) and Ian Learmonth (CEFC)

I established a high-trust Working Group model that met 64 times in a single year. The group was respected for being reliable, well-prepared, and transparent—even when decisions were contested. I also built a team culture that encouraged trusted, direct relationships with government stakeholders. We understood when to inform, when to consult, and when to deliver.

I haven’t yet worked with the WA Government, I am confident in my ability to build rapport quickly.

The principles—honesty, curiosity, and clarity—are universal. I would begin by understanding what matters most to WA leaders, then adapt our engagement style and narrative to reflect the State’s unique challenges and ambitions.

Energy Replacement

Transition sounds like we have time, it is vague and non specific.

I would like to explore a more impactful story telling message than the ‘energy transition.’

Language and words are important. Something I have learned as CEO of Marinus Link is the frustration of the average person around what transition and transmission are and its importance and relevance and urgency.

Global best practice isn’t something you admire from afar. It’s something you adapt, act on, and build coalitions around. That’s how I lead.

I stay informed through curiosity and action. I don’t just read the reports—I build the relationships, lead the projects, and ask, “What would world-class look like in our context?”

I am not one to shy from asking for help or seeking information from other energy businesses.

When looking to justify the spend on Marinus and accessing deep Hydro storage, I reached out to Snowy Hydro and asked for any of their modelling of the 2022 Energy Crisis, to support valuation of storage in times of crisis.

As an aside, deep and long duration storage should be measured and valued on an insurance type basis. A concept I have been sharing with all energy participants. This is a longer conversation on energy market reform around structure.

I would regularly connect or have the team regularly connect with global OEMs, infrastructure investors and system operators to stay informed. Being informed can then lead to shaping of strategy. At Marinus Link we leaned on our Engineering contractor Jacobs to connect us with other global HVDC project teams. We met teams from Viking Link and the Champain Hudson Power Express.

We mostly wanted to test our understanding of the tight cable and converter market to ensure we were not putting undue pressure on governments to act quickly.

After signing deals with Prysmian and Hitachi, we kept informed on markets and changes through our relatioships with our suppliers.

At Marinus Link, I built the business case for a multi-billion-dollar transmission asset in a fast-moving global HVDC market, aligning three governments and private suppliers on timing, value, and delivery certainty. That took more than knowledge—it took leadership under pressure, with clarity, composure, and conviction.

Innovation

Innovation isn’t just about technology—it’s about mindset, structure, and scale.

What I bring is system-level innovation.

At Hydro Tasmania, I re-framed deep storage as essential for a decarbonised NEM. At Marinus Link, I created new commercial pathways—like Australia’s first capacity reservation agreement—to overcome supply bottlenecks and unlock progress.

At Synergy, I’d bring:

  • Flexibility thinking: Making the most of customer, grid, and market-side levers to reduce peak demand and firm supply.
  • Capital innovation: Structuring long-term commercial models that attract investment while aligning with government priorities.
  • Execution culture: Focusing on strategy that lives in the day-to-day, not just in presentations.

But there’s one frontier I believe we must start preparing for now: climate adaptation.

We are unlikely to meet the 1.5–2°C target. That means we must start thinking beyond decarbonisation and begin preparing for operating energy systems in a hotter, more volatile world—systems with greater bushfire risk, water variability, load volatility, and equipment stress.

I don’t yet have all the answers. But I know we need to start asking better questions— and Synergy should lead that conversation in WA.

Many of the answers to the energy transition will be technology-driven. But only if we think systemically—and act commercially.

Take Kaluza, for example—a UK-based platform spun out of OVO Energy. It’s one of the most advanced orchestration systems I’ve seen. It intelligently manages 30,000+ EVs, optimising charging based on day- ahead pricing, capacity market signals, and system constraints. It doesn’t just respond to signals—it anticipates them. That’s the kind of adaptive technology thinking we’ll need at Synergy, especially in a constrained grid like WA’s. I met with the CEO Mel Gander last week to talk about what she is seeing in the market from a technology perspective.

Vision

What I bring is system-level innovation.

My vision for Synergy is to lead Western Australia through the replacement of legacy energy systems with new, resilient, and intelligently designed infrastructure—while keeping the customer, people, performance, and purpose front and centre.

  1. Ramping up renewables

    This isn’t just about building—it’s about orchestration. I bring experience sequencing firming, storage and capital to deliver what the system actually needs.

    Platforms like Kaluza show what’s possible—using data and flexibility to optimise system outcomes in real time. To make this work in WA, Synergy may need to collaborate in ways it never has before—with partners, competitors, and adjacent industries—coordinating infrastructure, capability and timing to deliver grid-scale outcomes. That kind of leadership isn’t technical—it’s cultural.

  2. Replacing coal with dignity and clarity

    This is not just about decommissioning—it’s about honouring the people and places behind legacy generation. I’ve led respectful, staged exits before—ensuring those involved walk out with pride and that culture isn’t left behind. The work is commercial—but the impact is human.

  3. Elevating current operations

    You don’t get to shape the future unless you’re delivering today. I’ve lifted engagement, aligned exec teams, and driven performance by connecting people to purpose. That culture of accountability-with-care is exactly what sustains a high-performing base business.

  4. Future-building with foresight

    WA’s system will face climate, technology and market shifts that aren’t yet fully priced in. My vision includes preparing for a hotter, more volatile future. Not just decarbonisation—but system adaptation. That’s the next competitive edge—and Synergy should lead the charge.

Comms and Crises

Communicating with Clarity, Credibility and Calm

As the primary spokesperson for Synergy, I would approach media and government engagement with three key principles: prepare deeply, communicate authentically, and respect the audience’s perspective.

I take time to understand the context—the business, the politics, the market, and most importantly, the people. I build what I call a “cheat sheet” of facts and figures I can refer to with confidence, especially in high-pressure interviews or when there’s conflicting commentary in the media.

But I don’t just communicate data—I craft the story. People connect with stories. One of the more nuanced parts of my communications approach—especially at Marinus Link—was getting the narrative right for each audience.

When speaking at a conference in Devonport, I’d anchor the message in Tasmania’s deep hydro legacy, referencing local history and the role the state played in powering early industry. In Gippsland, I’d draw on coal heritage and community resilience—how generations built and sustained the system that powered the state. It wasn’t always easy to get the Comms team to see that storytelling was strategy—but over time, they did.

Alongside storytelling, I worked with the team to build a robust, scalable comms framework: collateral banks, scenario maps, ministerial briefings, escalation channels, and contingency plans for media and stakeholder engagement. With three governments overseeing Marinus Link, alignment and discipline were non- negotiable.

Briefings for government were always clear, factual, and supported by internal experts—but delivered in my own voice, with or without the Chair depending on the need.

One of my proudest moments was leaving an ABC radio interview with Leon Compton, after a critical announcement. David O’Byrne, the former Opposition Leader, turned to me and said:

“That’s the first time I’ve actually understood what Marinus does.”

That moment proved to me that clarity isn’t just nice to have—it’s leadership.

Crisis management is where clarity and preparation matter most.

At Hydro Tasmania, one of our major industrial customers threatened to leave the state—sparking a serious economic and political challenge. As a GBE, we had fiduciary duties to act commercially, while also fielding government pressure to compromise and managing intense stakeholder gamesmanship.

We established a cross-functional crisis team that included comms, legal, risk and strategy. But most importantly, we gave government confidence. That meant bringing them into the process early, sharing structured plans, rehearsing scenarios, and making sure the story we told externally and internally held together. It wasn’t just managing the issue—it was leading through it.

That’s how I show up in a crisis: prepared, principled, and clear.

Wholesale & Retails

Aligning Retail & Wholesale for Enterprise Success—Even with Structural Boundaries Retail and wholesale are often structurally and culturally distinct—and at Synergy, the wholesale business is ring-fenced by legislation. But that doesn’t mean they can’t be strategically aligned in service of the customer and the state.

At Hydro Tasmania, I helped close the transfer pricing gap between wholesale and retail (Momentum), working closely with Amy Childs, MD of Momentum. We shifted from internal margin protection to enterprise value creation—anchored in what was best for the customer.

And I’ll be honest—transfer pricing is always a point of tension, across sectors. As founder of a retailer, it was a regular conversation with our first investor, Darryl Flukes. These aren’t theoretical issues; they affect real customers—their prices, reliability, and trust.

My approach:

  • Start with customer outcomes

    • What do customers need: price certainty? reliability? green choice? Retail and wholesale should align around those needs—even if their mechanics differ.

  • Respect the ring-fence—but unite on intent

    • Structural separation doesn’t mean strategic fragmentation. Leadership must drive toward shared goals that deliver public and customer value.

  • Create feedback loops

    • Use retail customer insight—usage patterns, price sensitivities, preferences—to inform generation and firming strategies upstream.

  • Design pricing and risk-sharing that reflects system value

    • That means building contracts between retail and wholesale that recognise flexibility, time-shifting, and firming—not just energy volume.

This approach builds cohesion and commercial strength—but most importantly, it keeps the customer at the centre, even when parts of the business are structurally separated.

Commercial

Balancing Commercial Priorities with a Values-Driven Culture

For me, there’s no contradiction between strong commercial outcomes and a collaborative, values-led culture. In fact, I’ve found they reinforce each other.

I’ve often worked in environments—like Marinus Link or Hydro Tasmania—where commercial performance had to align with government expectations and public trust. That’s where I draw on one of my favourite ideas: “long-term greedy”, coined by Goldman Sachs’ Gus Levy.

It means doing your homework, keeping your word, honouring relationships, and always doing the right thing —even when no one is watching. That mindset balances short-term decisions with long-term integrity. It’s a commercial philosophy with values at the core.

Real-world example: HVDC Cable Capacity Reservation Agreement

When global supply chains were collapsing, I led the development of an entirely new commercial structure to secure HVDC cable and converter capacity for Marinus Link. We got three governments and CEFC aligned by:

  • Creating a transparent, shared-risk model
  • Getting the Commonwealth to underwrite the reservation
  • Giving suppliers certainty without locking the project prematurely

It was capital efficient, aligned to stakeholder needs, and built long-term trust.

Culture matters too

I’ve never achieved strong commercial results without building culture first. At Marinus, I led with clarity, authenticity, and strengths-based leadership. As a result, we hit tight commercial milestones and lifted engagement—because people felt part of something real.

Commercial outcomes follow when your people are aligned, empowered, and backed by a leader who knows when to hold the line—and when to walk beside them.

Waste Reduction - Not Cost-Cutting but Unblocking Value

I don’t come at value from a “cut costs” mindset. I focus on freeing up time, capital, and energy to reinvest in what really matters.

That means:

  • Automating the repeatable so talent can focus on the strategic, In a waste reduction program at Hydro, our C&I team programmed robots (AI) to fill in customer data, reducing error and human’s doing repetitive work.
  • Removing friction—bureaucracy, unclear decision rights, siloed processes. Going back to strengths, clarity
  • Simplifying complexity so people aren’t navigating red tape to deliver. I have found at Hydro and Marinus closed thinking and processes that don’t match the decade.

This isn’t austerity. It’s empowerment.

And the real upside comes not from cutting—but from growing.
